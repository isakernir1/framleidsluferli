Laser cut, press fit wooden box.

The second project of the semester was to design a press fit object, using the 3D modeling software Fusion 360. The model was to be parametrized, that is, easily user-scalable in terms of both kerf and dimensions.

During the course of this project I utilized two programs in order to sufficiently complete the task at hand. They are the following, Fusion 360 and InkScape.

Before working on this project my knowledge of Fusion 360 was none, however I have some knowledge of Inventor so I decided to dive right in. My first mistake was the parametrization—or the lack thereof—which I realized before too long. Thankfully, Hafliði had provided us with some introductory videos to Fusion 360 and so I watched the first one before moving any further, in which both parametrization and figer-joints were explained in detail.

After a failed first attempt, I could start the designing process. After some internet search, I found the inspiration on this website: Boxes.py. First things first, parametrization:

As can be seen on the image above, I have created sveral parameters for the model, which enables me to reference said parameters when determining dimensions for the model, as opposed to using strict dimensions for each and every body/object created.

Now I was ready to design my model, starting with the bottom plate of the box.

And as per the instructional video, I created a new sketch on the desired face and extruded it inwards to make the first female finger-joint.

To save time and prevent human error, I selected the "Rectangular pattern" option under "Create" to multiply the extrusion and evenly distribute them over the corresponding face, over a distance calculated as shown in the above image. I used this method throughout the entire designing process.

The next step was to create some the front- and backend of the box. I began by sketching a rectangle of a predetermined dimensions on the outer-face of the jointed edge of the bottom plate. I then extruded the sketch inward. To save time, I then combined the two objects such that the finger-joints on the bottom plate remained and the corresponding sections of the back-end were cut (see "Edit Feature" box in right hand image).

Because the box is essentially symmetrical I could extrude the existing back-end to the other side, forming the front-end of the box. The extrusion is extended to any face of the opposing finger-joints (circled in the above image) and then a second extrusion is made in the opposite direction, giving the wall thickness. The finger-joints, of course, I created in the same manner as explained above.

The side panels were created in the same way as the front- and backend: A sketch is created on one side, extruded and antother extrusion is extended to a face on the other side, which again is extruded into a panel.

On the inside of one of the side panels I created a sketch and extruded it to a wall. I then made some careful calculations for the further cutting of the newly created wall, as can be seen in the left and center image. As can then be seen in the right image, I extruded the wall to the other side like before.

For the top sliders, I created a sketch on the top face of one of the side panels, which I then extruded down and combined the two, using the side panel as the cutting tool. This I extruded to the other side, too.

For the separator or the mid panel I created a sketch underneath the bottom plate and extruded it upwards.

I then made the same cut as I did on the inner side panels. The finger-joints were made as before, although, instead of making the first cut a fixed length from the edge, I made the first cut right on the edge.

Here you can see the finger-joints on the mid panel.

Lastly, I made a lid, which slides underneath the top sliders.

...And a small handle for the lid

This is the final design!

Before I could laser cut the design, I had to set it up in a printable format and determine the KERF of the laser cutter to be used. And before I can do that, I first had to make a copy of my completed design and then, using the "Joint" command under the "Assemble" option, moved the box, piece by piece on top of the plain rectangular body, making sure nothing overlapped and all faces were correct.

This is what it looks like after I have taken the copy apart and each body has been moved to its place.

The last thing I did in Fusion 360, before I moved to InkScape, I created a sketch on top of one of the bodies' faces and offset every circumference of each body about half of the decided KERF. I then exported this sketch as a .DXF file. I opened the new .DXF file in InkScape and made sure everything was correct after which I exported the drawing as a .PDF for the laser cutter.

This is the end result.
