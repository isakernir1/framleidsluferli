Milling something smaller: A negative mold.

The fifth project of the semester, like the previous one, was part of a larger group project. This project again involved milling, however, this time a negative mold. Unfortunately, because of how the pandemic has escalated, a few weeks ago we recieved the sad news that the allotted time for the project would not suffice and that there would inherently be some cut-backs. For this project we decided on something we were all familiar with and we thought to be easily executable, a LEGO block.

We decided to divide the project among ourselves so each of us only oversaw a part of it. My contribution to the project involved designing the block. (The Lego block's toolpaths can be found here.) During the course of this project I utilized only one program: Fusion 360.

By now I have spent countless hours in front of the screen, rapidly navigating the monolithic interface offered by Fusion 360 and, frankly, become quite fond of it. This is now the third project of the semester that I almost entirely complete in Fusion 360 and I, therefore, required no introduction nor warm-up before I embarked on this concluding journey.

As so many of my previous endevours, this one, too, began with a simple square. Then extruded to form a base for my design.

Next, on the newly formed extrusion, I sketched six circles, equal in size, and extruded them. For what is a LEGO block without its tabs? A block.

I then created a new working plane through the center of the block and extruded it two ways in order to create the base for the ultimate mold.

I combined the "mold" and the block, after which I split in half, along the new working plane.

The "mold" is now in two parts so to ensure a pleasant casting experience, I added alignment tabs. First on one corner, which I then multiplyed and placed on each of the four corners, using the "Rectangula Pattern" option.

The casting hole is an important component of every negative mold. That is why I included one on this one. First, a rectangular sketch on one of the smaller sides that I then extrude inwards to cut out material to the inside.(We had some complications with the conic configuration in the toolpath preperation and instead chose the afformentioned rectangular geometry.)

Lastly, I followed an instructional video provided and created by the course instructor on how to finalize the two part mold. I selected one of the two halves of the mold and selected the "Align" option from the "Modify" tab. For compatability reasons I moved the two parts slightly apart.

I finally created a new sketch in the working plane I had previously created, about 10mm longer on all sides than the mold itself and extruded it, as last time, both upwards and downwards. The final outcome can be seen in the right-hand image.
